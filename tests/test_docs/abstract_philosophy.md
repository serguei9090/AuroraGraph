# Shifting Paradigms: An Exploration of Abstract Theories

## Epistemological Frameworks

### The Gettier Problem
In 1963, Edmund Gettier published a three-page paper titled "Is Justified True Belief Knowledge?" that fundamentally shattered the traditional definition of knowledge. Before Gettier, knowledge was universally defined as "Justified True Belief" (JTB). Gettier provided two specific counterexamples (often involving a man named Smith, a job interview, and coins in a pocket) where a person has a justified true belief, but intuitively lacks actual knowledge because their belief happens to be true due to luck. If Smith believes the man who gets the job has 10 coins in his pocket (because he was told Jones would get the job, and counted Jones's coins), but unknown to Smith, *he himself* will get the job and also happens to have 10 coins, his belief is true and justified, but is not knowledge.

### Phenomenology and Intentionality
Edmund Husserl's phenomenology centers on the concept of "intentionality"—the idea that consciousness is always *about* or *directed toward* something. You cannot simply "think"; you must "think of" an object. Martin Heidegger later expanded this in "Being and Time" (1927) with the concept of "Dasein" (Being-there). Heidegger argued that before we abstractly analyze the world, we are always already thrown into it, engaging with tools (ready-to-hand) rather than objectively observing facts (present-at-hand). When a hammer works, we don't think about it; we only notice it as an object when it breaks.

## Theories of Time and Persistence

### Endurantism vs. Perdurantism
The metaphysical debate about how objects persist through time is divided into two main camps.
- **Endurantism:** Claims that an object is entirely present at every moment of its existence. If an apple exists on Monday and Tuesday, the *whole* apple exists on both days. It sweeps through time.
- **Perdurantism (Four-Dimensionalism):** Argues that objects are extending through time just as they extend through space. An object has "temporal parts." The apple on Monday is just one temporal slice of a four-dimensional "apple-worm" that stretches from the time it grew on the tree to the time it is eaten.

### The Ship of Theseus
Plutarch first recorded this thought experiment: If the wood of a ship decays over time and is replaced plank by plank until no original pieces remain, is it still the same ship? Thomas Hobbes later added a complication: What if someone gathered all the discarded original planks and rebuilt a second ship? Which one is the *true* Ship of Theseus? The continuity of form competes against the continuity of matter.

## Semantic Externalism
Proposed by Hilary Putnam in his 1975 essay "The Meaning of 'Meaning'", semantic externalism argues that meaning is not entirely in the head. He introduced the "Twin Earth" thought experiment. Imagine a planet identical to Earth in every way, except the clear liquid they call "water" is not H2O, but a complex chemical XYZ. When an Earthling says "water" and a Twin Earthling says "water," they are in the exact same psychological state, but their words have different meanings because they refer to different external substances in the real world. Thus, Putnam concluded, "meanings just ain't in the head."
